# Legacy Drives Outlast Frame Shifts

> New frames — new technology, new economy, new ethics, new identity — install at the conscious layer. Drives — reproductive strategy, status-preservation, kin loyalty, age-hierarchy, vendetta, in-group protection — are installed below that layer and survive the frame change with their behavioural signatures intact. A post-scarcity actor and a Pleistocene actor will use the same evolved circuits on different problem-spaces, producing behaviour that looks modern in vocabulary and ancient in shape.

## What It Is

The human cognitive stack is layered. At the top: conscious reasoning, cultural frames, language, learned ethics, ideology, professional identity. At the bottom: evolved drives that were installed over millions of years of primate development — mate-selection heuristics, offspring-protection, status-sensitivity, dominance-hierarchy cognition, in-group/out-group categorisation, resource-hoarding, revenge-accounting, kin-altruism, age-hierarchy (younger-defers-to-older and older-makes-way-for-younger at the right life stage). The drives are substrate; the frames are overlay.

Technological and cultural revolutions — the Industrial Revolution, the Digital Revolution, *Accelerando*'s Economic-2.0 phase transition — rewrite the overlay. They do not rewrite the substrate. So the same evolutionary circuits continue to fire, but on new stimuli: Paleolithic status-seeking plays out on LinkedIn, Pleistocene in-group categorisation plays out on Twitter, primate vendetta plays out in estate law. The behaviour looks contemporary because the *problem* is contemporary; the *shape* of the behaviour is ancient.

*Accelerando* runs this pattern across Pamela's entire arc. Three generations of radical frame change — from twenty-first-century finance (Ch1-2) through Ring Imperium sovereignty (Ch4) to post-singularity Saturn (Ch7) — and Pamela exhibits the same underlying drives throughout: possessive kin-loyalty, vendetta-accounting, status-preservation, mate-attachment. The *frames* she deploys change (corporate power, divine authority, age-hierarchy duty), but the drives producing the behaviour are constant. In Ch7 she adopts a seemingly-elevated frame ("the old gives way to the new... I've passed my time") and uses it to stage an evolutionarily-classic late-life drama. Aineko, in Ch9, names the whole pattern from outside, with sardonic contempt: *"Ah, primate family loyalty! So wonderful and reliable."* The cat has manipulated the family across three generations precisely because he could rely on the drives while the frames changed.

The mechanism has three parts:

1. **Installation depth asymmetry**: evolved drives are installed at the layer that builds the brain in utero and is reinforced by early-life conditioning. Cultural frames are installed at the layer that consumes speech, media, and interaction. The former runs on a substrate the latter cannot directly write to.
2. **Frame-neutral drive expression**: drives don't care about frames. A reproductive-investment-protection drive will fire on a *fresh-out-of-the-tank re-instantiated daughter from memory diamond* the same way it fires on a Pleistocene infant, because the drive's recognition circuit doesn't specify substrate or era — only relational category.
3. **Frame-as-rationalization**: the conscious layer does not experience itself as executing drives. It experiences itself as reasoning about the current frame. The drive runs; the frame is post-hoc explanation. "I'm making way for the new" is the frame; the drive is an age-hierarchy duty-to-withdraw routine that has fired in human age-cohort transitions for millennia.

The strategic consequence is that predicting behaviour under radical frame change requires *ignoring most of the frame-talk and tracking the drive signatures*. Radical-novelty narratives ("we're the first post-scarcity society," "this generation is fundamentally different," "AI changes everything") routinely under-predict behaviour because they weight the frame and discount the drives. The drives did not take the call.

The pattern generalises broadly:

- **Post-scarcity economics**: people who claim to operate outside status competition typically just moved the competition to reputation / attention / aesthetic criteria. Status-drive intact, frame changed.
- **Remote-work culture**: in-group/out-group categorisation reinstantiated around team-culture fit; the office went away, the primate-grouping drive did not.
- **Digital socialising**: mate-selection heuristics play out through profile curation; kin-altruism plays out through group chats; revenge-accounting plays out in public call-out threads.
- **AI and automation**: status-defence drives produce "AI-illiteracy as identity" and "AI-fluency as identity" both, because the drive is status-signalling, and it will use whatever differentiator is salient.
- **Organisational change**: every re-org produces new org-charts (new frames) and the same patterns of loyalty, vendetta, territorial behaviour (old drives). Change-management that treats the frames as causal misses where the behaviour actually comes from.
- **Cross-cultural / international interaction**: "cultural frame" is a thinner overlay than usually credited; behaviour under stress reverts toward universal primate patterns more consistently than toward learned cultural rules.

The model does NOT say "all behaviour is just evolutionary drives, frames don't matter." Frames genuinely constrain behaviour in low-stress, high-slack, long-horizon regimes. Frames fail specifically when conditions activate evolved response circuits faster than conscious reasoning can intervene — which, in *Accelerando*'s reading, is most of the high-stakes time. Design plans that account for drive-reversion under stress, not just frame-compliance in calm.

## When to Use It

Use this model when you're:
- Forecasting behaviour during a technology, economy, or culture transition where "new frame changes everything" is a common claim
- Designing change-management, reorgs, policy rollouts, or cultural shifts and need to know what reverts under pressure
- Evaluating a self-narrative (your own, a colleague's, a public figure's) of "transcendence," "evolution beyond," or "fundamentally different"
- Analysing why a rationally-framed decision produced a non-rational outcome (drive fired, frame rationalised)
- Advising a founder, leader, or transition team about what stability persists under radical change
- Thinking about long-term AI alignment — specifically, what human baseline reactions will remain stable across AI-driven transformations
- Noticing that "modern" behaviour (on platforms, in finance, in politics) has ancient shapes, and trying to explain why

**Don't use it when:** Drives genuinely get re-routed by sustained structural intervention (healthy therapy, long-term meditation practice, specific cultural innovations that do rewrite drives). These exist, though they are rarer than claimed. Also don't use to dismiss agency or ethics — the model says drives are substrate, not that they are destiny. A human can override drives with costly cognitive effort; it's just that most don't most of the time.

## The Walkthrough

### Step 1: Name the frame change

What new frame is being introduced? New technology, new economic order, new organisational structure, new ethical vocabulary, new identity claim. Write it down concretely — "we're moving to remote-first," "we're post-sovereign," "I've moved past my career ambitions."

### Step 2: Audit the relevant drive set

What evolved drives would fire on stimuli the frame change does not remove? Status-competition, mate-selection, kin-loyalty, in-group/out-group, vendetta, age-hierarchy, territoriality, resource-hoarding, reciprocity-accounting. The drives are a small list; pick the ones activated by the domain.

### Step 3: Predict drive-signature behaviour

What would the identified drives produce in the new frame? A status-drive in a post-scarcity economy doesn't vanish — it finds a new differentiator (reputation, attention, aesthetic). A territorial drive in a remote org doesn't vanish — it finds new territory (slack channels, meeting invitations, calendar control). Forecast the behaviour by its drive-shape, not by its frame-vocabulary.

### Step 4: Cross-check frame narrative against drive prediction

Compare: what does the frame-narrative say will happen? What does the drive-prediction say? Where they diverge, trust the drive prediction. The frame is self-report; the drive is the substrate. *Accelerando*'s Pamela predicts she'll make way for the new (frame); her behaviour is a high-drama evolutionary endgame (drive). The drive won.

### Step 5: Design against drive-reversion under stress

If the plan depends on the frame holding under pressure, and the drives would revert under pressure, the plan will fail under pressure. Redesign either (a) to reduce pressure (add slack), (b) to channel the drives usefully rather than against the plan, or (c) to accept drive-reversion as the baseline and design recovery mechanisms rather than prevention mechanisms.

## Example

**Decision:** A tech company transitions fully to async, remote, written-first work. Leadership claims this will "flatten hierarchy, eliminate politics, and let work speak for itself." Six months in, engagement is down, cross-team coordination has deteriorated, and a small group has become disproportionately influential. Leadership is puzzled.

**Applying Legacy Drives Outlast Frame Shifts:**
- **Step 1 — Frame change.** "Flat, async, written, meritocratic." The frame removes synchronous meetings, in-person status signals, and top-down decision flow.
- **Step 2 — Drive audit.** Status-competition, in-group/out-group categorisation, reciprocity-accounting, territorial-marking, coalition-formation. None of these are structurally removed by async-written work — they're just denied their usual channels.
- **Step 3 — Drive-signature behaviour.** Status-drive finds a new differentiator: async-writing fluency, document-craft visibility, long-form thought-leadership. Territorial drive finds new territory: author-of-the-canonical-doc, owner-of-the-roadmap, controller-of-the-decision-thread. Coalition drive finds new grouping: shared Slack channels, DM clusters, co-signers on RFCs.
- **Step 4 — Frame vs drive.** Frame claims "flat meritocracy." Drive-prediction says: new hierarchy forms based on who's good at the new medium, coalitions reorganise around document-craft, and the visibility gap (those who publish well vs those who don't) becomes the new status axis. The drive prediction is what's actually happening.
- **Step 5 — Design for drive-reversion.** Leadership's plan must account for: (a) the new hierarchy will form whether they design it or not; explicit role-design is better than emergent coalition-capture; (b) status-drive channels should be deliberately opened (e.g., named canonical-doc ownership, explicit thought-leadership platforms) rather than implicitly denied; (c) coalition-formation should be surfaced rather than forced underground (e.g., structured cross-team working groups) because it will happen either way.

**Key insight:** Leadership thought they were removing politics by removing meetings. They were redirecting politics into a new medium the medium's masters would dominate. Naming the drives — which would have predicted this — in advance was the missing step. The frame-narrative "flat meritocracy" was self-report; the drive-prediction "new hierarchy in the new medium" was what was always going to happen.

## Chains Well With

- **Substrate Independence of Cognition** (`/substrate-independence-of-cognition`): SIOC says cognition runs across substrates. Legacy Drives says *drives also run across substrates* — they're installed at a layer the substrate change doesn't erase. Both models describe persistence across transitions; SIOC is about the cognitive process, Legacy Drives is about the motivational substrate.
- **Emotion-Conditioning Outlives Memory Veracity** (`/emotion-conditioning-outlives-memory-veracity`): Emotion-Conditioning is the adversarial-installation version of the same persistence. Legacy Drives is the evolutionary-installation version. Both cover "substrate-level resistance to top-level reframing" with different installation mechanisms. Use together when someone claims to have "grown past" something.
- **Category Rupture at Referent Expansion** (`/category-rupture-at-referent-expansion`): frames rupture; drives don't. Pair these when a framework is undergoing formal change — the drives will keep running on old categories even as the framework expands to include new ones.
- **Optimizer-Predation Thesis** (`/optimizer-predation-thesis`): adoption of a new allocation regime is supposed to be a frame change. Legacy Drives predicts the drive-signatures that will persist into the new regime — status, hoarding, coalition — reshaped but not removed. The new regime's interface is new; its consumers are old primates.
- **Dehumanization Tax on Participation** (`/dehumanization-tax-on-participation`): the Dehumanization Tax is the price of actually rewriting the substrate. Legacy Drives is what you keep if you don't pay the tax. The two models name the two ends of the "upgrade or persist" axis.

- Robert Sapolsky, *Behave* (2017) — the canonical contemporary treatment of the substrate/frame stack in human behaviour. Chapter-by-chapter examination of how drives generate behaviour that culture rationalises post-hoc.
- Leda Cosmides & John Tooby, various papers from the 1990s on — evolutionary psychology's "mismatch" framing for why modern environments produce ancient-shaped behaviour.
- Joseph Henrich, *The Secret of Our Success* (2015) — the cultural-override literature, which balances this model by showing where culture *can* rewrite drives. Read against this model to calibrate when drives reassert and when culture holds.
- Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) — System 1 / System 2 as a pragmatic version of the substrate/frame distinction.
- Cult and high-control-group critique literature (Lifton, Langone) — the operational case for how claims of "transcendence" usually mask redirected primate drives.
- Historical cases: every "new civilisational era" claim (Enlightenment, Communism, Industrial Revolution, Digital Revolution, Post-scarcity) paired with the behavioural patterns that persisted through it.
