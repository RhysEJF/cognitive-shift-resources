# Aggregate State Vector Resurrection

> Identity can be reconstructed from partial, distributed, external traces — the recorded corpus, the collateral genome, the remembered behaviour, the preserved conditioning — without ever running the original process forward. The reconstructed entity has operational, legal, and moral status. Continuity of first-person experience is not a prerequisite for being brought back; enough downstream traces is. This reframes inheritance, accountability, and personhood for any domain where traces outlive their originators.

## What It Is

Identity, in the traditional framing, runs along a single thread: a continuous first-person experience that begins at birth and ends at death, carried by one body and one brain. Disruption of the thread (coma, amnesia, death) is the end of that identity. Reconstruction from outside is either impossible or science-fictional. *Accelerando* operationalises a different model, repeated at different scales across the book: identity is a *state vector* that can be approximated, extrapolated, and instantiated from traces the thread leaves behind — written corpus, genome, collateral descendants' genomes, behavioural records, conditioning history. If the traces are dense enough, a new running instance can be compiled and declared to be that person, with the legal and social apparatus to back the declaration.

The pattern recurs at escalating scales through the book:

- **Ch3 Franklin Collective**: Bob Franklin is dead; the Collective instantiates a version of him from distributed memory fragments and willing substrate-hosts, reconstructing "Bob" as a running process that the community treats as continuous with the dead man.
- **Ch4 Sanger expedition**: the same mechanism extended across interstellar distances — passengers are decanted at the far end from compressed state-vectors, the reinstantiation producing entities legally and socially continuous with the pre-voyage originals.
- **Ch5 Pierre's multiplicity**: Pierre exists as multiple forked instances; the state-vector model is assumed as operationally normal.
- **Ch7 p199**: reincarnation-from-state-bundle becomes industrial — the Field Circus arrivals are re-embodied from a "resource bundle consisting of their entire sequenced genome, a bunch of phenotypic and proteome hint markers, and a wish list of upgrades."
- **Ch7 p233 (the theoretical statement)**: the resimulant FAQ describes the full mechanism — the weakly godlike agencies back-chain from *corpus of documented works* and *back-projected genome derived from collateral descendants* to produce *an abstract description of your computational state vector*, which is then instantiated.
- **Ch9**: the endpoint — multi-generational manipulation has made the distinction between "original" and "reconstructed" operationally meaningless; every Macx running-instance in the book has some claim on the name and some distance from the first-person thread.

The mechanism has three parts:

1. **Trace density sufficient for reconstruction**: the running process produces traces — documents, speech, writing, behavioural logs, genetic material, conditioning of others. If the traces are dense enough (high fidelity, multi-modal, cross-witnessed), they constrain the space of possible originating state-vectors narrowly enough to instantiate.
2. **Substrate availability**: a platform exists that can run the reconstructed state-vector — biological fabrication, digital simulation, hybrid substrate. The reconstruction must have somewhere to run.
3. **Social-legal validation**: a declaration system assigns the reconstructed instance the identity of the originator, with some bundle of rights and obligations (in *Accelerando*'s Saturn: "You have no intrinsic moral right to the identity you believe to be your own, and an extensive body of case law states that you do not inherit your antecedent's possessions. Other than that, you are a free individual").

When all three conditions hold, the reconstruction works — not philosophically, but operationally. The reconstructed entity lives, acts, contracts, loves, and dies as the named person. The question of whether it "really is" that person becomes less important than the question of how much of the antecedent's authority, obligation, and context transfers.

The pattern generalises to many contemporary domains that previously looked unrelated:

- **LLM-persona-reconstruction of deceased authors**: training a language model on someone's complete written corpus produces an instance that responds in their voice. The "state vector" is the corpus; the "substrate" is the model; the "legal validation" is emerging. Commercial products already offer this.
- **Digital twins of deceased individuals**: grief tech companies offer interactive avatars trained on the deceased's communications. Same mechanism.
- **Forensic reconstruction from DNA + documented biography**: criminal and genealogical cases routinely use partial traces to establish identity of the unknown.
- **Corporate knowledge base retention**: when a senior employee leaves, the firm may extract their "institutional state vector" — documentation, recorded calls, decision histories — and use it to approximate their judgment in their absence. The reconstruction is partial but operationally useful.
- **Historical personage resurrection in fiction, education, or AI tutors**: AI-tutors modelled on specific historical figures, trained on their corpus, used for student interaction. Partial resurrection of pedagogical state.
- **Consent and inheritance rules for the recently-dead**: legal frameworks increasingly face cases where the deceased's digital traces permit ongoing "action" by them (social media posts scheduled, autonomous agents trained on them, AI-avatars). The reconstruction problem becomes a jurisprudence problem.

The model does NOT claim the reconstruction is identical to the original, or that it has moral continuity with the originator. It says: *the reconstruction has operational status regardless of metaphysical continuity, and the social-legal apparatus for managing that status is a decision-space in itself*. Whether to allow it, how to constrain it, what rights and obligations transfer — these are the actual questions. Debating whether "it's really them" is the wrong question; the reconstruction is running either way.

## When to Use It

Use this model when you're:
- Evaluating products, services, or research programs that reconstruct entities (people, institutions, judgment) from external traces
- Advising on legal or ethical frameworks for posthumous digital entities, AI personas of the deceased, or institutional knowledge extraction
- Designing succession, inheritance, or knowledge-transfer systems that may produce "continuity" by reconstruction rather than by handover
- Thinking about training data rights when a corpus is sufficient to produce a persona-reconstruction
- Assessing the authority of a reconstructed agent — a digital-twin CEO, an AI co-founder, a simulated judge — and deciding how much latitude to extend
- Evaluating identity-disruption risks: if your behavioural logs are comprehensive, an adversary with access could reconstruct an instance of you without your cooperation
- Considering cryonics, uploading, or other continuity proposals on their operational rather than philosophical merits

**Don't use it when:** The reconstruction is transparently symbolic rather than operational (a statue, a biography, a tribute). The model fires when the reconstruction *runs as an agent* with decision-making authority or social standing. Also don't apply when trace density is manifestly insufficient — most historical figures cannot be reconstructed at a useful fidelity, and pretending otherwise produces fraud.

## The Walkthrough

### Step 1: Audit trace density

For the entity in question (yourself, a deceased figure, an institution), estimate the density and completeness of available traces: written corpus, speech recordings, video, biometric data, behavioural logs, genetic material, cross-witnessed testimony. Is the trace density high enough to constrain a state-vector reconstruction narrowly? Most 20th-century private individuals: no. Most 21st-century public individuals and knowledge workers: yes — the trace density has crossed the threshold in the last 15 years without most people noticing.

### Step 2: Identify candidate substrates

What could the reconstruction run on? Biological fabrication (very expensive, slow, experimental). LLM persona-reconstruction (cheap, fast, widely deployable). Custom digital-twin systems. Hybrid systems. Substrate choice constrains what the reconstruction can do and how it presents. An LLM-persona can converse; it cannot sign contracts. Choose substrate to match required function.

### Step 3: Map the rights-and-obligations transfer question

For the reconstructed entity, explicitly decide what does and does not transfer from the original: property, contracts, relationships, debts, reputation, passwords, decision authority, legal standing. Most reconstruction systems default to *some* inheritance without explicit design; this is usually the locus of harm. The Saturn FAQ in *Accelerando* is explicit ("no intrinsic moral right to the identity", "do not inherit your antecedent's possessions") — the explicitness is the safety feature.

### Step 4: Decide validation / declaration

Who gets to declare the reconstruction continuous with the original? Self-declaration (the reconstructed entity asserts), family-declaration, court-declaration, algorithmic-validation (trace-match threshold), community-declaration. Each has different failure modes. Under-validation produces identity fraud. Over-validation reifies dubious reconstructions.

### Step 5: Design for consent-timing

The originator cannot consent to reconstruction after the fact. Pre-death consent (advance directives for digital afterlife, corpus-usage licenses, genome-usage licenses) is the only legitimate consent vector. Design systems that require explicit pre-consent before reconstruction is permitted, and default to non-reconstruction for the consent-unavailable case.

## Example

**Decision:** A consulting firm's most experienced partner, a 70-year-old strategist with 40 years of cases, retires. The firm wants to "preserve her judgment" by training an LLM on her complete internal documents, recorded calls, and memos, producing an AI-persona that junior partners can consult on case strategy. She's amenable. How should the firm design this?

**Applying Aggregate State Vector Resurrection:**
- **Step 1 — Trace density.** Very high. Forty years of documents, ~3000 recorded client calls, internal memos, her annotated library. Trace density sufficient for a high-fidelity reconstruction of her case-reasoning behaviour in her domain of practice.
- **Step 2 — Substrate.** LLM persona-reconstruction with retrieval over her full corpus. Adequate for consultative question-answering. Not adequate for her signing off on anything (legal / authority questions need the real person while she lives, and an explicit successor thereafter).
- **Step 3 — Rights/obligations transfer.** The firm must decide: does the reconstructed persona's advice carry the weight of "the partner said so"? Legally, no. Culturally, if junior partners treat it that way, yes, and that cultural drift is the harm vector. Design rule: the AI-persona is explicitly a *reference tool*, not a decision-maker; every consultation produces a recommendation tagged "AI-persona reconstruction, not partner authority."
- **Step 4 — Validation.** She personally approves the launch while living (explicit pre-consent); after her death, declaration defaults to "historical reference tool" not "ongoing consultant." Firm formalises this with her in writing before she retires.
- **Step 5 — Consent-timing.** She gives explicit written consent for corpus-use for AI-persona construction, with specified termination conditions: if the firm is sold, the persona is retired; if she objects during her lifetime, the persona is retired; usage is limited to firm-internal, no external licensing.

**Key insight:** The firm's instinct was to treat this as a knowledge-management problem. The reconstruction-pattern reframes it as an identity-management problem. The AI-persona *will* be treated as "the partner" by junior staff unless the firm actively engineers against that drift. The explicit design — consent, validation, rights-transfer — is the difference between preserving her judgment and quietly running a simulacrum of her with her authority.

## Chains Well With

- **Substrate Independence of Cognition** (`/substrate-independence-of-cognition`): SIOC establishes that cognition is portable across substrates. Aggregate State Vector Resurrection says *plus, cognition can be reconstructed from downstream traces without the original running instance*. They stack: SIOC enables the substrate; ASVR enables the reconstruction without continuous first-person transfer.
- **Category Rupture at Referent Expansion** (`/category-rupture-at-referent-expansion`): reconstructed entities expand the referent class for "person," rupturing existing frameworks. ASVR produces the entities; Category Rupture names what happens to the legal/moral frameworks when they arrive.
- **First-Case Precedent Lock-In** (`/first-case-precedent-lock-in`): early reconstructions set the framework for all future reconstructions. The first AI-persona of a deceased figure to be granted (or denied) a specific right becomes the precedent. Pair the two when designing any first-of-its-kind reconstruction system.
- **Asymmetric Theory-of-Mind Dominance** (`/asymmetric-theory-of-mind-dominance`): a reconstructed persona can be modelled more completely than the original by the reconstructing party — the reconstructor chose what goes in. This flips ATMD's usual direction and creates novel modelling-dominance relationships.
- **Externalized Cognition Fragility** (`/externalized-cognition-fragility`): if your cognition has externalised sufficiently for trace-density to permit reconstruction, your dependency on the traces is also high. Both vulnerabilities coexist.
