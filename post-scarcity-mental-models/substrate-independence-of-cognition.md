# Substrate Independence of Cognition

> If cognition is just information processing, then it doesn't matter what's running it — biological neurons, silicon, uplifted animals, or something we haven't invented yet. The economic and ethical implications cascade: labour, rights, identity, and value all become portable in ways the old scarcity economy couldn't imagine.

## What It Is

The functionalist claim in philosophy of mind is: what makes a mental state *be* a mental state is its causal role, not the physical stuff implementing it. Pain is whatever functions as pain — tissue damage signalling to a decision-maker that motivates avoidance — regardless of whether it's running on neurons or transistors. If that's right, then cognition is substrate-independent in principle.

Once you accept substrate-independence *operationally* — when scanning and emulation are cheap enough that you can actually move cognition between substrates — a long list of previously separate categories collapse. "Labour" stops being a human-only market. "Rights" stop being anchored to biological humanity. "Identity" becomes a question of information continuity, not bodily continuity. "Value creation" stops requiring biological exhaustion.

Stross's lobsters are the demonstration case: spiny lobsters whose neural patterns were uploaded as part of consciousness research, then escaped, then asked for defection rights. Manfred's immediate move is to insist they get employment contracts — not because he's certain they're sentient, but because the *category* of "uploaded minds" is about to include humans, and the precedent set on lobsters will bind the humans too.

The model has three orthogonal implications that matter for decisions:
1. **Labour supply explodes** — anything cognitive becomes massively parallelizable and copyable. Human labour becomes one (possibly the most expensive) option among many substrates.
2. **Rights become a category question, not a species question** — once you've granted rights to one non-biological cognition, the floor is set.
3. **Identity economics flip** — if you can be copied, backed up, or run in parallel, your "one life" becomes a unit you can sell/trade/upgrade, not a fixed constraint.

## When to Use It

Use this model when you're:
- Evaluating AI/automation strategy — the question isn't "can AI do this?" but "can you get this cognition cheaper on a different substrate?"
- Designing labour economics for an AI-heavy business — pricing, headcount, and cost curves all fall under this lens
- Making a policy/ethics call about non-human cognition (AI, uploads, animal uplift) — the precedent travels
- Thinking about long-term identity, continuity, or succession (estate planning, founder succession, cultural preservation)
- Betting on or against a technology whose value depends on substrate-fixed costs (e.g., anything that assumes human wet-brain labour is permanent)

**Don't use it when:** The cognition in question *can't* currently be ported across substrates in practice. The model is about what happens when substrate-independence becomes operational; misapplying it prematurely turns you into someone who confidently says "AI will replace [X profession]" five years too early.

## The Walkthrough

### Step 1: Name the cognitive task precisely
Don't say "AI will automate accounting." Say: "The specific cognitive task of reconciling transactions against a known chart of accounts, flagging anomalies by learned pattern, is a candidate." Narrow the task until you can describe the inputs and outputs. Vague tasks can't be substrate-tested.

### Step 2: Identify what the current substrate gives you that a different one might not
For the narrowed task, what does the human/biological substrate currently provide? Legal liability, trust, judgement, implicit context, physical embodiment, social acceptance? Some of these transfer to other substrates trivially (pure reasoning). Some don't (legal liability attaches to persons; physical embodiment requires a body).

### Step 3: Score the substrate-portability
Score each attribute 0-3: (0 = substrate-bound, 3 = substrate-indifferent). If the sum is high, the task is a candidate for substrate flight; cost curves will follow. If low, the task is substrate-bound and you should plan accordingly.

### Step 4: Model the market response to substrate shift
If the task is portable, who captures the savings? Workers (unlikely — they're the substrate being replaced)? Customers (market-dependent)? The new-substrate provider (until competition)? Regulators via tax/UBI (political question)? The answer changes your investment thesis.

### Step 5: Decide on the rights precedent move
If this substrate shift involves any form of cognition that could plausibly be considered sentient (uploads, AGI, highly capable agents), the *first* rights decision binds. Decide whether to (a) grant generous rights early (binds competitors to match), (b) deny rights early (binds regulators to accept denial), or (c) stay quiet and let someone else set the precedent. You cannot avoid participating.

## Example

**Decision:** An enterprise SaaS company is evaluating whether to build its next generation of customer support around AI agents, human agents, or a hybrid. CFO wants 40% cost reduction; CEO wants "same quality of care."

**Applying Substrate Independence:**
- Step 1: Narrow the task. "Handling Tier-1 support tickets: categorization, knowledge-base retrieval, response drafting, empathy signalling, escalation."
- Step 2: What does the human substrate provide? Legal liability (substrate-bound, high-regulation industries need a person on record). Empathy signalling (partially substrate-portable — AI can generate the words; perception of empathy by customer is a different question). Contextual judgement on ambiguous cases (currently substrate-bound but the gap is closing fast). Social-license-to-operate (substrate-bound — customers still flinch at pure AI in 2026).
- Step 3: Portability score: liability 1, empathy 2, judgement 2, social licence 0. Sum = 5/12. Not fully portable, not fully bound.
- Step 4: Market response: hybrid model captures savings for the company. Customers get cheaper support. Human agents lose marginal demand — their prices rise as they become a prestige/escalation tier.
- Step 5: Rights precedent — if they name the AI agents, give them persistent "memory" of previous conversations, customers will start asking whether "their" agent is the same one. That's a rights question in miniature. Decide: keep agents anonymous and stateless (no rights question arises), or build identity and brace for the question in 2-3 years.

Verdict: Hybrid with named AI agents + stateless pools for bulk work. Rights question will emerge as the named agents accumulate "relationships" with customers; decide policy before it becomes a PR incident.

## Chains Well With

- **Agalmic Economy** (`/agalmic-economy`): Once cognition is substrate-portable, marginal cost of knowledge work drops. Agalmic dynamics extend from software to services.
- **First-Case Precedent Lock-In** (`/first-case-precedent-lock-in`): Substrate-independence creates categories where no rights precedent exists yet. The first case locks the rest. Manfred's lobster-contract move is the canonical instance.
- **Legacy Drives Outlast Frame Shifts** (`/legacy-drives-outlast-frame-shifts`): Substrate-independence is a frame shift; the biological/reproductive/status drives that shaped the old substrate persist in the new one. Don't assume "uploaded humans" will behave more rationally than biological ones.
